The Investment Structure
Discovery
FIH Group PLC — AIM-listed UK conglomerate. Three segments: FIC (Falklands essential services, £29.0m rev), Momart (institutional art logistics, £19.2m rev), PHFC (Gosport Ferry, £4.2m rev). Two loss-making sub-streams — FBS Construction and Momart Exhibitions — suppress the blended optic to 3.9x EV/EBITDA. The market sees a mediocre conglomerate. We see three separable monopoly assets priced as one.
Catalyst
In March 2026, the board sold PHFC to Collins River / Uber Boat at an implied EV of £15.2m — 12.6x pre-IFRS 16 EBITDA. Sale proceeds of £11.6m flow to balance sheet, compressing post-disposal EV to £15.4m. This is a closed transaction, not a projection. A strategic buyer independently validated the multiple on a monopoly concession. The remaining portfolio (FIC + Momart) can be acquired at an implied 7.1x net acquisition cost / rump EBITDA (or 3.8x on post-disposal market-implied EV).
Control Strategy
Acquire control at 30% premium (£43.6m equity, sensitivity range 20–50% — see § 7.3). Strip FBS and Exhibitions (£3.8m one-time cost). Post-strip rump EBITDA of £4,098k (post-disposal, pre-IFRS 16). Net effective acquisition cost on post-PHFC EBITDA: 7.1x. Base case 5-year hold: ~3.5x MoIC / ~28% IRR at 10x exit. Bear case (8x): ~2.5x / ~20%. Triple-stress scenario (FBS £6m + EBITDA to £3m + SLB reprice): 1.7x / 11% — capital preserved but returns fail. Post-PHFC cash yield on sponsor equity: 8.0%; payback 12.4 years; interest coverage 4.3x.

The Gap

PHFC was sold at 12.6x. The remaining portfolio is available at 7.1x net acquisition cost (3.8x on post-disposal EV). The rump businesses (FIC, Momart) have stronger monopoly characteristics than PHFC. FIC has operated as the sole essential-services provider in the Falkland Islands for 175 years. Momart has stored institutional art for the Tate, V&A, and Royal Collection for 51 years with near-zero client churn. The 5.5-turn gap between the PHFC sale (12.6x) and rump entry (7.1x) is the structural mispricing.

What the Comps Tell Us

Art logistics is consolidating. Iron Mountain (27x EV/EBITDA) has made 4+ art storage acquisitions since 2015. TSG Consumer Partners ($200–800m equity cheques) took majority control of Cadogan Tate in 2022 and immediately executed 3 bolt-ons. Momart is the leading UK player in a market both strategic (IRM) and financial (TSG) buyers are actively consolidating. It is not an orphaned asset without a buyer universe.

Ferry concessions attract infrastructure capital. Northleaf (Canadian pension-backed, $25bn AUM) took majority control of Thames Clippers in 2022, then backed the Gosport Ferry acquisition at 12.6x. This is not a one-off — it’s a thesis by an institutional infrastructure investor that UK passenger ferry monopolies are attractive assets.

Multiples: Specific transaction multiples for art logistics M&A are largely undisclosed. However, Iron Mountain trades at 27x EV/EBITDA as a public company; Cadogan Tate’s TSG acquisition was reportedly in the mid-market PE range ($200–800m equity). The 12–16x exit range for Momart Storage is conservative relative to IRM’s public trading multiple and the demonstrated PE appetite.

§ 6.1 — Shareholder Analysis

The Article 6 Marital Trust — Zucker Holding

28.7% beneficial holding. Represented on the board by R.B. Johnston. The trust structure and precedent transactions materially reduce the “Zucker will not sell” risk.
Trust Structure
Entity: The Article 6 Marital Trust, created under the First Amended and Restated Jerry Zucker Revocable Trust dated 4-2-07.
Trustee: Anita G. Zucker (Chair & former CEO, The InterTech Group, Inc.)
Holding: 3,596,553 ordinary shares = 28.92% (now ~28.7%)
Board representative: R.B. Johnston (Chief Strategy Officer, InterTech Group)
Trust type: Marital trust (Article 6 = QTIP-style provision). Created after Jerry Zucker’s death (2008) from his revocable trust, which became irrevocable on death.
Key implication: Anita Zucker has sole voting, investment, and dispositive power over the shares. She can authorise a sale unilaterally as trustee — no beneficiary consent required for investment decisions.
Precedent: Zucker Has Agreed to Sales Before
The original pitch flagged “Zucker will not sell” as the highest-severity risk. However, SEC filings reveal the trust has repeatedly entered irrevocable undertakings to support takeover transactions:

Fyffes / Sumitomo (2016): The Article 6 Marital Trust gave an irrevocable undertaking to vote in favour of Sumitomo’s £751m cash acquisition of Fyffes plc. Johnston sat on the Fyffes board as InterTech’s representative — identical structure to FIH.

FR Bison Holdings (2016): Zucker entered a Support Agreement with FR Bison, agreeing to vote in favour of the merger and approval of the merger agreement at every stockholder meeting.

Bowl America / Bowlero (2021): Zucker actively opposed a takeunder she considered undervalued, calling it an “egregious takeunder” and considered launching her own competing bid — demonstrating she is a price-sensitive, engaged investor, not a passive holder.

Pattern: Zucker sells at fair value. She fights undervaluation. A 30% premium offer at 348p (vs. 268p) through Johnston — her own board representative — has a credible path.

Revised Risk Assessment

The “Zucker will not sell” risk is materially lower than originally assessed. The trust structure grants Anita Zucker sole dispositive authority. She has entered irrevocable undertakings to support M&A transactions in at least two precedent situations (Fyffes/Sumitomo, FR Bison). She has actively fought undervaluation (Bowl America). The FIH holding at 268p represents ~£9.6m — immaterial relative to her ~$1.9bn net worth. The gating test remains: open channel via Johnston (Weeks 1–4, zero cost). But the base case should assume she is receptive to a fair-value offer, not that she blocks the transaction.

Why Pre-IFRS 16 Is the Correct Operating Lens

1. The Leyton SLB rent (£750k p.a.) is a real cash cost already in operating expenses. IFRS 16 capitalises it into “debt” that cannot be called, refinanced, or accelerated.

2. The PHFC sale at 12.6x was computed on a pre-IFRS 16 basis (per RNS). Same basis for the rump = apples-to-apples.

3. However: a lender WILL see the IFRS 16 liability. Debt covenants will reference it. This must be disclosed to any financing counterparty.

4. The IFRS 16 EV of 7.5x is still attractive vs. the 12.6x PHFC benchmark — the thesis holds on both bases, it’s just less dramatic.

§ 7.2 — FIG Lease Risk

FIG/Crown Land Leases — The Hidden Structural Risk

This risk is CAUSED by the acquisition itself. A private PE acquirer changes the political calculus.
KEY FLAG — REQUIRES PRE-IC DILIGENCE
FIC occupies Crown/FIG land in Stanley at likely subsidised rents. The West Store, Homebuilder, vehicle yard, and port facilities all sit on government land. Note: FIC also owns 400 acres of land and 82+ residential/commercial properties outright, so the exposure is partial, not total.

Argentine Precedent (2017): When Argentine businessman Eduardo Elsztain indicated interest in a takeover bid for FIH, FIG explicitly stated that any proposed change of ownership affecting Falklands assets would be “scrutinised for compliance with Falkland Islands law.” The bid was vigorously resisted by shareholders and FIG. This confirms that FIG considers itself a gatekeeper on change-of-control events affecting FIC. (Source: Falkland Islands Association)

A private profit-maximising PE acquirer is not Argentina — but the political dynamic is analogous. FIG has 3,000 residents and one essential-services provider. The original Value Creation Pillar C (“Monopoly Pricing Actions”) proposed raising prices on captive islanders — this is politically toxic and should be replaced with operational efficiency measures (see revised Pillar C below).

Mitigant: FIC owns substantial property outright (400 acres). Crown land leases may have long tenure. The key is to approach FIG as a partner, not an adversary — emphasising investment, job protection, and service continuity. A UK-domiciled mid-market sponsor with a community-partnership narrative is far more palatable than an Argentine conglomerate or an offshore fund.

Actions required BEFORE IC: (1) Obtain lease tenure details and change-of-control provisions. (2) Assess FIG consent requirements via Johnston channel. (3) Determine whether FIG scrutiny is statutory or political. (4) Price downside scenario: +£500k p.a. lease repricing = −£5m EV at 10x.
SOCIAL LICENCE TO OPERATE
The Guardian-headline test: “PE fund buys Falkland Islands monopoly, raises prices on 3,000 captive islanders.” This story writes itself and is the kind of reputational risk that mid-market sponsors increasingly screen for.

Revised Value Creation Pillar C (replacing “Monopoly Pricing Actions”):
• Supply chain optimisation: FIC ships goods via MOD FIRS vessels (10 sailings/year). Consolidating procurement, negotiating better freight rates, and reducing inventory holding periods could save £200–300k p.a. without touching consumer prices.
• Property yield improvement: FIC owns 400 acres and 82+ properties. Selective refurbishment, better tenant management, and vacancy reduction — all at market rents on owned land — is politically neutral. Estimated uplift: £100–150k p.a.
• Tourism ancillary revenue: 35,000+ cruise ship passengers visit annually. FIC operates the Capstan Gift Shop but is underweight on tourism services. Modest investment in tourist-facing retail and experiences is politically positive (brings money in from outside).
• Estimated total: £300–450k p.a. EBITDA improvement. Less than the original £375–535k but defensible and politically sustainable.

§ 7.6 — Exhibitions: Wind-Down vs. Trade Sale

The original pitch assumed wind-down. But TSG/Cadogan Tate is actively acquiring art logistics businesses. A trade sale could be NPV-positive.

Redundancy: ~20 installation crew × £30k avg = £600k base, £1m bear.

Equipment: Specialist rigging, climate crates. Limited secondary market. £150–400k.
In-flight project overruns: £0–500k.
Client relationship risk: Closing Exhibitions while retaining Gallery/Storage clients from the same institutions requires careful messaging.

Revised total: £1,150–3,200k (expanded from original £1,150–2,700k due to committed contract and stranded space analysis).
Path B: Trade Sale (Upside Scenario)
Cadogan Tate (TSG-backed): Active acquirer — bought Artemis (Dec 2023), Art Care (May 2022), Wetzel (Nov 2020). Exhibitions is complementary to their platform. UK museum relationships they currently lack. Est. price: £1–3m. Probability: Medium.

Crown Fine Art: Cadogan Tate competitor. Smaller, less acquisitive. £0.5–1.5m. Probability: Low.

Net swing vs. wind-down: Trade sale avoids £600k+ redundancy AND generates £1–3m proceeds = £1.6–3.6m NPV improvement. On a £12.7m equity cheque, this is material.

Action: Sound out Cadogan Tate / TSG during Weeks 8–16 (parallel to Momart client references). If no trade buyer, execute wind-down.
